High-Velocity 3D Printed Plane

A lightweight airframe tuned for high-g maneuvering, optimized infill, and reinforced stress points. Built to see how far a desktop 3D printer can go when you treat it like a wind-tunnel tool instead of a toy.

Role Design, printing, tuning, flight testing
Tools Fusion 360 · Voron 2.4 · Klipper
Focus Lightweight structure · stiffness · print speed

Overview

This plane started as a “can I print something that actually flies?” and turned into a full structural tuning project. I focused on keeping weight low while reinforcing the nose, wing roots, and tail so it could survive real landings and aggressive turns.

Instead of brute-force thickness, the design uses targeted walls and infill density. Low-stress regions are hollowed out, while high-stress regions get material and geometry where it actually matters.

What I built

  • Parametric fuselage and wing in Fusion 360
  • Reinforced spar and wing root geometry
  • Print profiles tuned for speed and adhesion
  • Modular nose section for repairs and experiments

Why it matters

The same thinking that kept this airframe light but durable applies directly to brackets, fixtures, and prototypes that have to handle load. It’s less about “cool plane” and more about building mechanical intuition around where a print will fail.

Stack
  • Fusion 360 (parametric CAD)
  • Voron 2.4 (coreXY printer)
  • Klipper + input shaping
  • PLA / lightweight PLA blend
Highlights
  • Large weight savings vs. naive solid prints
  • Survived repeated flights and rough landings
  • Balanced CG without adding dead ballast
Next iterations
